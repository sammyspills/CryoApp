Centre for Polar Observation and Modelling

The Centre for Polar Observation and Modelling (CPOM) is a NERC Centre of Excellence that studies processes in the Earth’s polar latitudes that can affect the Earth’s radiation reflectance, polar atmosphere and ocean circulation, and global sea level.
CPOM is funded by the Natural Environment Research Council (NERC). We also work closely with the British Antarctic Survey (BAS), National Oceanography Centre (NOC), National Centre for Earth Observation (NCEO) and European Space Agency (ESA).

Introducing CryoSat

Diminishing ice cover of oceans (sea ice) and on land (glaciers and ice sheers), is frequently cited as an early casualty of global warming. Ice plays an important role in regulating climate and sea level in various ways: Albedo, the ability of the Earth’s surface to reflect radiation, for example, affects warming of the oceans and decreases as sea ice extent decreases. The consequences of climate change are far reaching. It is therefore important to understand exactly how sea ice, glaciers, ice caps and larger masses like the Greenland and Antarctic Ice Sheets are responding to these changes – and by how much.
CryoSat is Europe’s first mission to specifically measure ice changes in these areas.
CryoSat was the first Earth Explorer mission to be selected in 1999, but the original satellite was lost due to a launch failure in October 2005. A successor, CryoSat-2, was subsequently built with a number of improvements and was launched on 8 April 2010.

About the Satellite

The satellite itself was commissioned by the European Space Agency and made in Germany by Astrium GmbH (now Airbus Defence and Space). It is equipped with state-of-the-art remote sensing technology, comprising of a Dual-Polarised Synthetic Aperture Radar Altimeter Interferometer specifically designed to overcome challenges in measuring the thickness of floating ice, as well as a Doppler Orbitography Receiver designed to precisely measure the position of the satellite and its orbit. It has an orbital period of approximately 99 minutes, and a precession period of 30 days. The satellite therefore returns a sparse scan of both the Arctic and Antarctic every 30 days, with the full updated scan being provided ever 369 days (After completing 5344 revolutions).

About the Research

CPOM uses theoretical and laboratory-derived information to form new models of interactions between the ice, ocean and atmosphere, and uses ground-based and satellite observations to test these and other climate models.

The CPOM Directorate is based at the University of Leeds, and we have researchers at the Universities of Bristol and Reading and at University College London.
